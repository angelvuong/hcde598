Assignment 6: Molding and Casting

I learned molding and casting for this week's assignment. The goal of this project was to design and fabricate a silicone mold and to create four identical casts. I was excited to return back to my screen block theme and to create my own tiny screen blocks out of plaster! For this assignment I casted blocks in the "Vista Vue" pattern.

Materials and Tools:
Rhino
3D printed mold
OOMOO 30 Silicone Mold Rubber
Plaster of Paris
Lots of paper towels, mixing containers, and cleaning supplies

Step 1. Designing the Mold

I designed my mold-mold in Rhino to be 3D printed. When we were assigned this project, I thought that I would mill out my design in wax. I learned from classmates that 3D printing was an easier way to go about doing this project since there were a lot of hiccups encountered with cutting the wax down to size/milling times. First, I modeled a "Vista Vue" block pattern (flat shapes, extruded) then I added it into a 2x2" box shape in Rhino.




Next, I exported my design as an STL file to be 3D printed.

Step 2. 3D Printing

I set up the printers to 230Â°F and set up printing settings in the Dremel Slicer. I set the infill at 20% and printing was straightforward. The print time was roughly 8 hours, and the first few layers of printing appeared to be successful, so I left the printer to do its work overnight. (This is why I love 3D printing!)




I returned the following day to pick up my mold. I was impressed by how it turned out (exactly as I wanted it to), considering I encountered many printing troubles on the third assignment.


Step 3. Silicone Molding

Follwing the package instructions for OOMOO 30, I mixed the two liquids together at a 1:1 ratio. I poured the combined mixture into my 3D printed mold (up to the brim) and allowed it to set overnight. Removing the silicone mold from the printed mold was very challenging! I wanted to be gentle with the mold because I worried that the inner parts would rip out. I tried prying the mold free with a butter knife and eventually got it out by pulling, prodding, pushing, and *not* ripping out the inner parts. Since I had plenty of leftover OOMOO, I also created a second mold.



Step 4. Plaster Casting

To create my plaster block, I mixed Plaster of Paris at a 2:1 (powder to water) ratio and poured it into my two silicone molds. I left the plaster to set for one hour. I was worried about removing the plaster from the mold and feared that the inner parts would get stuck/rip out/crumble, but the blocks came out very easily! Success! Overall, the end-to-end process was easy but time-consuming. I really enjoyed creating my own blocks and hope to revist this project in the future to create more block patterns. Since I have more than half of the box of plaster mix left, I plan to use it all up by casting as many blocks as I can. I plan to gift the blocks to my architecture friends.
